Understanding Autism

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a developmental condition that affects how people communicate, interact socially, and process information. It is a spectrum, meaning autism presents differently in each individual.

Autism is not a disease or disorder to be "fixed." Rather, autistic individuals have unique strengths, perspectives, and ways of experiencing the world. Understanding autism helps us build a more inclusive society.

Early identification and support can help individuals develop skills and strategies to thrive in their personal and professional lives. Many successful individuals are autistic and make valuable contributions to society.

Core Characteristics of Autism

Communication

Autistic individuals may communicate differently. Some are non-speaking, while others may be very verbal. Communication differences are a feature, not a flaw.

Social Interaction

Social processing may differ. Autistic people often have genuine interest in connecting but may approach relationships differently than neurotypical individuals.

Sensory Processing

Many autistic people experience heightened sensory sensitivity to sounds, lights, textures, or tastes. Accommodations and understanding help create inclusive environments.

Interests & Strengths

Focused interests and deep knowledge in specific areas are common. Many autistic individuals excel in pattern recognition, attention to detail, and specialized skills.

Routine & Predictability

Many autistic people thrive with structure and predictability. Transitions and unexpected changes can be challenging but are manageable with support.

Neurodiversity Perspective

Autism is a different neurotype, not a deficit. The neurodiversity movement celebrates autism as part of human diversity.
